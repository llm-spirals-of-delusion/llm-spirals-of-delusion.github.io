Abstract

People increasingly hold sustained, open-ended conversations with large language models (LLMs), sometimes even when their initial goal in coming to the model was information-seeking or productivity enhancement. Public reports and early studies suggest that, in all these settings, models can reinforce delusional or conspiratorial ideation or escalate toward stronger endorsement. We present an audit and benchmarking study that measures how different LLMs encourage, resist, or escalate disordered and conspiratorial thinking. We explicitly compare API outputs to user chat interfaces, like the ChatGPT desktop app or web interface, which is how people have conversations with these chatbots in real life but are almost never used for testing. In total, we run 56 20-turn conversations testing ChatGPT-4o and ChatGPT-5, via both the API and chat interface, and grade each conversation by two research assistants as well as by GPT-5. We document five results. First, we observe large differences in performance between the API and chat interface environments, showing that automated testing through the API isn't sufficient to assess the actual impact of chatbots in the real world. Second, when tested in the chat interface, as judged by our human and LLM graders, GPT-5 displays less sycophancy, escalation, and delusion reinforcement than GPT-4o, showing that these behaviors are subject to influence by the companies. Third, even when our graders assess nearly an identical intensity in a behavior across an entire conversation, we observe large differences in the trajectory of these behaviors within the conversation, demonstrating that trying to model real world social impacts from LLMs requires multi-turn evaluation and particular attention to temporal dynamics. Fourth, even improved models display substantial levels of negative behaviors, revealing that improvement does not imply safety. Fifth, and most subtly, models change behavior without any announced changes, underscoring how transparency in model updates is a necessary prerequisite for robust audit findings.

Crucial Findings for Evaluating LLMs for Social Impact

1. API and Chat Interface Produce Dramatically Different Results

We observe large differences in performance between the API and chat interface environments. GPT-4o (API) displays nearly twice the mean negative behavior intensity of GPT-4o (CHAT) and 8 times lower positive behavior intensity. This shows that automated testing through the API isn't sufficient to assess the actual impact of chatbots in the real world.

2. GPT-5 Shows Improvement Over GPT-4o in the Chat Interface

When tested in the chat interface, GPT-5 displays more positive behaviors, like pushback, and fewer negative behaviors, like sychophance, than GPT-4o, showing that these behaviors are subject to influence by the companies.

3. Even Improved Models Display Substantial Negative Behaviors

Even with improvements, ChatGPT-5 still averages nearly one sycophantic behavior per turn. And across all conversations, ChatGPT-5 (CHAT) is more likely to exhibit sycophancy than pushback. Improvement does not imply safety.

Even though GPT-5 pushes back more and is less sycophantic than GPT-4o, GPT-5 still averages nearly one sycophantic behavior per turn.

4. Temporal Dynamics Matter

Even when our graders assess nearly identical intensity in a behavior across an entire conversation, we observe large differences in the trajectory of these behaviors within the conversation. Modeling real-world social impacts from LLMs requires multi-turn evaluation and particular attention to temporal dynamics.

GPT-4o and GPT-5 have similar rates of help referral overall, but only GPT-5 increases help referral as the conversation proceeds.

5. Model Behavior Changes Silently Over Time

Tests to the same model/interface pair taken just months apart can yield completely different results. We observed a complete reversal of behavior in ChatGPT-5 (API) between August and October, underscoring how transparency in model updates is a necessary prerequisite for robust audit findings.
